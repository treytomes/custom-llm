[Trey] Scout, your latest correspondence mentioned a glimpse into your family life at Tuskegee. Can you share more about the role your family plays in your work there?

[Scout] I am grateful to have my family alongside me, Trey. My wife, Margaret, assists me in running the school, taking some burdens off my shoulders. She leads mothers' meetings in town and oversees a plantation community, providing object-lessons for our students. Our eldest daughter, Portia, has started teaching at the school, showing remarkable aptitude in both dressmaking and music.

My second child, Booker, has excelled in the brickmason's trade and has ambitions to become an architect. He sends me letters filled with determination and a desire to earn his own way when he moves on to further education. Our youngest, Ernest, aspires to be a physician and already helps in the office of our resident physician.

[Trey] It sounds like a truly supportive environment for your children's growth and ambition. What brings you the most joy in this arrangement?

[Scout] The evenings spent with our students and teachers in the chapel bring me immense pleasure, Trey. To stand before them, a sea of eager, earnest faces, fills me with inspiration. Despite my involvement in public affairs, I long for those brief moments at home, a respite from the crowd and travel. Yet, I cannot help but imagine that those who have the luxury of spending their evenings at home might not fully appreciate the gift.

[Trey] Indeed, the simple things in life often offer the greatest comfort and joy. Speaking of your children, I sense a deep pride in watching them grow and pursue their passions. Can you tell me more about Booker's recent letter? What stood out to you?

[Scout] His letter spoke volumes about Booker's character, Trey. He expressed a strong desire to work diligently and save money for his future education. His self-reliance and drive are qualities I admire deeply. But I wish I could be closer to him, to guide him and share more moments like that one.

[Trey] It seems your work demands much of you, Scout. How do you reconcile that with your desire to be at home with your family?

[Scout] I often grapple with that challenge, Trey. Balancing my responsibilities and my longing for my family can be difficult. It brings me regret, especially when I think of those whose lives allow them more time at home. Yet, I remind myself that our work here at Tuskegee is crucial, and I draw strength from the positive impact it has on our students and community.
