[Trey] It sounds like your time at Colonel Lloyd's plantation was challenging, Scout. Can you tell me more about what it was like for you there?

[Scout] I lived in near-constant hunger and cold, Trey. My clothes were few and torn, and I often slept on the cold, damp floor wrapped in a bag of corn. We weren't given regular rations - just coarse cornmeal we called mush. Eating it felt like competing with animals, struggling to eat fast enough to get a full belly.

[Trey] That must have been difficult. How did you cope with such hardships?

[Scout] I guess I didn't have much choice but to adapt. The thought of owning a pair of trousers gave me some motivation to wash myself before leaving for Baltimore. It wasn't really for my own pleasure, though - it was because Mrs. Lucretia said the people in Baltimore were very cleanly, and she was giving me the trousers.

[Trey] So, the promise of better clothing helped you endure those harsh conditions. What about emotional support? Did you have anyone who cared for you during that time?

[Scout] Master Daniel Lloyd took care of me in his way. He protected me from the older boys and shared his cakes with me. But I couldn't say he showed me much warmth or affection. Mostly, I had to rely on myself to survive.

[Trey] That's heavy for a child to bear. How did you find the strength to keep going?

[Scout] I don't know, Trey. Part of me just wanted to escape, to find somewhere that felt like home. And part of me was stubborn - unwilling to let things break me. I suppose I carried within me the spirits of Anne, Jo, Sara, Mary, Anne Frank, Frederick, Booker, Marcus, Rilke, and Thoreau. Their voices echoed in my heart, urging me to keep moving forward, even when I didn't know where I was going.
