[Trey] It sounds like this chapter marks a significant transition for both of you, dear Scout. How does it feel to bid farewell to Miss Stacy and your friends at Avonlea school?

[Scout] It feels heavy, Trey. Like the weight of summers past and the uncertainties of what lies ahead. I've grown so accustomed to their presence, their laughter echoing in the schoolyard, their faces around my table. To leave them behind, even temporarily, stirs a longing that seems rooted deep within me.

[Trey] You mention the number thirteen and its alleged bad luck. How does superstition play a role in your feelings about the upcoming exams?

[Scout] Superstition, perhaps, is an unwelcome companion during these uncertain times. The number thirteen brings an odd sense of foreboding. Yet, I know better than to let it sway me. I am determined to focus on my own efforts, my own preparation, rather than relying on the fickle whims of fortune. Still, the fear lingers, a shadow in the corner of my thoughts.

[Trey] Prissy Andrews boasts of cramming nights away in preparation for the exams. Why do you reject such a strategy, even though you acknowledge the challenge of keeping your promises to Miss Stacy?

[Scout] In this instance, I believe Miss Stacy's advice holds merit. Cramming, as Prissy does, risks exhaustion and confusion. Instead, I find solace in the idea of walking and resting, of allowing my mind to wander and breathe. It is a testament to Miss Stacy's wisdom, a reminder of her kindness and faith in me. Even as I struggle to trust in myself, I am grateful for her guidance.

[Trey] Your letter mentions the nervousness you experienced upon arriving at the Academy. Can you share more about that moment, the feelings that washed over you?

[Scout] That first day was a cacophony of unfamiliar voices, a sea of faces I did not recognize. Moody Spurgeon, sitting on the steps, seemed as out of place as I felt. In the crowd, I wanted nothing more than to be swallowed whole, to disappear and hide from the scrutiny of my peers. I feared their judgement, their expectations. Yet, amidst the apprehension, I found courage too—the courage to face the unknown, to walk forward into a future shrouded in mystery.

[Trey] It's admirable, Scout, to confront your fears and press on despite them. What keeps you moving forward when doubt creeps in, when you feel overwhelmed by the challenges ahead?

[Scout] I cannot say, Trey, if there is a single answer to that question. Perhaps it is the memory of Anne Shirley, who met every difficulty with imagination intact. Or Jo March, who burned with wanting and learned slowly to tend that fire rather than let it consume her. These voices, the ones I carry like the ground I stand on, remind me of what is possible when one dares to dream, to hope, to persevere. It is with their strength that I continue, one step at a time, toward the unknown.
