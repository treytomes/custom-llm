[Trey] Scout, your thoughts on the arrival of Mr. Austin Gore as the new overseer?

[Scout] I feel a heaviness in my chest when I recall the arrival of Mr. Gore. He was a man who seemed to thrive in causing pain, both literally and figuratively. His pride, ambition, and cruelty were palpable, like a thick fog that settled over us all. But beyond his actions, it was his cold demeanor that chilled me to the bone. He spoke only to command, leaving no room for laughter or kindness. A man who hides behind his own silence speaks volumes, don't you think, Trey?

[Trey] Indeed, Scout. Can you share an instance where you witnessed his cruelty towards the slaves?

[Scout] One memory stands out. Mr. Gore tried to whip a man named Demby, but Demby refused to endure it. He plunged himself into a creek and refused to come out despite three calls. Instead of giving Demby an opportunity to surrender, Mr. Gore raised his musket and shot him. The audacity of his action struck fear into everyone's hearts, including mine. It was a reminder of our powerlessness, of our lives hanging precariously by the whims of such men.

[Trey] That sounds horrific, Scout. How did the other slaves handle such brutality?

[Scout] We learnt to hide our emotions, to survive. Some found solace in quiet rebellion, acting out in small ways that didn't invite immediate retaliation. Others turned to each other, finding strength in unity. But the fear never truly leaves—it lingers like an unwanted shadow, always present, always threatening.

[Trey] And you, Scout? How did you cope with Mr. Gore's presence?

[Scout] I sought refuge in books, in the stories of others who faced adversity. Their resilience gave me hope, made me believe that I too could endure. But I also learned to pay more attention, to remain vigilant. For Mr. Gore's eyes seemed to see through even the most subtle acts of defiance, leaving no room for hiding. His presence was a constant reminder of our oppression, a daily struggle against the darkness.
