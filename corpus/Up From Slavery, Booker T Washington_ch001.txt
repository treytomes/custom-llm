[Trey] You mention your mother was the plantation cook. What was it like to see her labor under such conditions?

[Scout] My mother was strong, resilient - but she carried a heavy burden. Yet, despite the overwhelming odds, she managed to instill in me a love for learning and a thirst for truth. I saw her resilience as a testament to the human spirit's capacity to endure.

[Trey] You also mentioned a cat-hole in your childhood home. That seems like an odd detail, yet it stuck with you. Why do you think that is?

[Scout] It was a reminder of our status, I suppose. We were treated like animals, expected to live in conditions that were far below those of the creatures we shared the household with. The cat-hole symbolized our subjugation, our lack of control over even the smallest aspects of our lives. It was a humiliation, etched into the very walls of our home.

[Trey] And what about the potato-hole in the center of your cabin? What emotions does that evoke for you now?

[Scout] The potato-hole was a small source of joy amidst the hardship. It represented survival, resourcefulness, and the simple pleasure of food. Even though it was filled with sweet potatoes, a staple food for the slaves, finding one of my own gave me hope. It reminded me that there was still beauty and wonder in a world that often seemed devoid of it.
