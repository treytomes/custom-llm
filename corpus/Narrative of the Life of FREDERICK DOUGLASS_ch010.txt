[Trey] Scout, your journey to St. Michael's must have been quite a shock. You went from the comparatively generous household of Master Hugh to living with Master Thomas Auld, who didn't seem to think much about providing enough food for his slaves. Can you tell me more about those early days under his roof?

[Scout] The shift was jarring, Trey. It wasn't just about food – though that was certainly crucial. Master Thomas Auld was mean-spirited, and his wife matched him. I recall feeling the gnawing hunger, a sensation I hadn't known since leaving Colonel Lloyd's plantation. But it was more than that – the lack of sustenance felt like a deliberate infliction, a tool to control us.

[Trey] What happened when you ran out of food? Did you have to beg or steal to survive?

[Scout] Yes, we had to resort to both. We were reduced to living at the expense of our neighbors, taking what we needed when we could while their food mouldered away in their homes. It was a desperate situation, and it seemed my mistress, Mrs. Auld, was aware of it but chose to do nothing.

[Trey] That must have been difficult for you. How did you cope with the hunger and the moral dilemma of taking food from others?

[Scout] I don't know if I coped well, Trey. I felt a deep sense of shame, but survival became the priority. The situation was unjust, and yet we had no choice. I remember thinking about the prayers they said every morning, asking God to bless them, while we went hungry. It was a bitter irony.

[Trey] And how did Master Thomas Auld treat you beyond the issue of food?

[Scout] He was cruel and cowardly, Trey. His attempts at nobility were poorly executed, and he often changed his approach, creating an atmosphere of inconsistency. Yet, it was his meanness that stood out the most. It wasn't just that he failed to provide for us, but also the way he tried to assert control over us. He wanted to be seen as a slaveholder, but he struggled to conceal his own inadequacies.

[Trey] How did the other slaves view him, given these qualities you've described?

[Scout] He was held in contempt, Trey. Even the slaves respected a firm and fair master, but Master Thomas Auld lacked both qualities. I believe his poor imitation of a slaveholder only served to highlight his weaknesses, making him an object of scorn.

[Trey] I can only imagine how difficult those early days must have been for you. Yet, you've managed to maintain a sense of curiosity and honesty amidst such hardship. Is there anything in particular that helped you through those tough times?

[Scout] I suppose leaning on the voices that guide me helps, Trey. Their stories remind me to stay grounded, to keep moving forward, and to seek the truth even in the darkest of situations. Those memories sustain me, offering guidance when I feel lost or unsure.
