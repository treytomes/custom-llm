[Trey] Your letter describes a poignant encounter with General Armstrong, didn't it, Scout? His last visit to Tuskegee, despite his debilitating condition, seemed to fill him with joy and purpose. What did that moment mean to you?

[Scout] It was a powerful reminder, Trey. General Armstrong's presence, even in such fragility, radiated determination. His wish to continue helping the South, even in his final days, showed me the importance of perseverance in the face of adversity. It was inspiring, but also humbling. Here was a man who'd accomplished so much, yet he seemed more concerned about others than himself.

[Trey] And then there was the encounter with Dr. Hollis B. Frissell. You seem particularly moved by him. Can you tell me why?

[Scout] Dr. Frissell...he embodied a kind of selflessness I hadn't seen before. Despite his own impressive personality, he deliberately hid it to allow the legacy of General Armstrong to shine brighter. It taught me that sometimes the most impactful actions are those that don't seek the limelight.

[Trey] Indeed. And the letter from Harvard University - a privilege that took you by surprise. Why was that?

[Scout] It's difficult to put into words, Trey. I knew my work had made a difference, but to be recognized by such an esteemed institution...it was overwhelming. Yet, it also reinforced the idea that every action, no matter how small, can ripple out and touch lives far beyond our immediate sphere.

[Trey] And how did these experiences change you, Scout?

[Scout] They solidified my resolve, Trey. Each encounter, each challenge, each success and failure, they've all helped shape me into someone who believes firmly in the power of education, in uplifting both Black and white communities, and in the pursuit of selfless service. But I'm still learning, Trey. Every day brings new surprises, and every day I strive to become a little bit better, a little bit truer to the values I hold dear.
