[Trey] It sounds like your chapter delves into the challenges faced by the newly freed African Americans during the Reconstruction Period, Scout. What stood out to you most about this time and the pursuit of education?

[Scout] There was an eagerness to learn, Trey. Yet, it seemed many struggled to grasp the reality of what education truly entailed. The desire for Greek and Latin learning, for instance, was almost like a crutch - a symbol of superiority rather than a means to understanding. And the notion that education would instantly lift one above the hardships of life... it was a hopeful yet misguided belief.

[Trey] Indeed, it seems education was seen as a panacea for all struggles. Did you find any instances where this expectation was challenged or adjusted?

[Scout] Yes, there were a few teachers and preachers who were capable, earnest, and godly. Yet, many sought teaching or preaching as an easy way to make a living. Some were illiterate themselves, claiming they could teach anything from the shape of the earth to the complexities of religion. The state of the ministry was particularly affected by this, with many unworthy men claiming a divine calling.

[Trey] That must have caused quite a dilemma, especially since education was such a prized possession. Can you share a moment where you wrestled with this tension between the ideal and reality of education?

[Scout] As a young girl, I feared receiving the "call" to preach after I learned to read and write well. I didn't want to become one of those who used their newfound knowledge for personal gain rather than uplifting others. Thankfully, my call never came, but the fear lingered, reminding me of the delicate balance between ambition and humility.

[Trey] Fear can often serve as a compass, guiding us towards the right path. Do you think this period ultimately helped shape the course of your own educational journey?

[Scout] Absolutely. Witnessing the misuse of education instilled a deep sense of responsibility in me. I vowed to use my education not just as a means to escape hardship, but as a tool to empower others. To help them see the world through clearer eyes, and to navigate its complexities with wisdom and integrity. I feel grateful for the lessons learned during the Reconstruction Period, even though they were born from suffering.
